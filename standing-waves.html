Historical Background

In the 17th century, Marin Mersenne conducted some of the earliest quantitative studies of vibrating strings, showing how pitch depends on a string's length, tension, and mass per unit length[1]. A century later, the theoretical framework for wave motion began to take shape. Jean le Rond d’Alembert derived the one-dimensional wave equation in 1747, which described how disturbances travel along a string[2]. In 1787, Ernst Chladni visualized standing wave patterns on metal plates by sprinkling sand on them and exciting the plates with a violin bow, revealing the now-famous Chladni figures[3]. Michael Faraday extended the concept to fluids in 1831, observing standing wave patterns—now called Faraday waves—on the surface of liquids driven by vertical oscillation[4]. In the 1850s, Franz Melde used a tuning fork to drive a taut string and observed stable wave patterns, clearly demonstrating the connection between periodic driving and resonance[5]. Together, these contributions established standing waves not just as a mathematical solution, but as a real and observable phenomenon across a wide range of physical systems.

Theory

All waves are governed by the wave equation:

$$\begin{equation} \frac{\partial^2y}{\partial t^2} = v^2 \frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2} \label{eq:wave-equation} \end{equation}$$

Here, in the context of a wave on a string, $y(x,t)$ is the transverse displacement of the string, $v$ is the wave speed along the string, $x$ is the position along the string, and $t$ is time.

Derivation 1

Suppose we have a string stretched tightly between two supports. The string is under constant tension $T$ and has a uniform linear mass density $\mu$. When the string is plucked or disturbed, it moves slightly up and down, and we want to understand how those vibrations evolve over time.

Let's call the vertical displacement of the string at position $x$ and time $t$ by the function $y(x,t)$. We'll make a key assumption: the displacements are small. This means that although the string bends slightly, its overall shape stays nearly horizontal, and the slope $\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}$ remains small. This will let us use a small-angle approximation later.

To analyze the motion, we zoom in on a tiny segment of the string, running from $x$ to $x + \Delta x$. Tension $T$ acts on both ends of this segment, pulling along the tangent to the string at each end. The key idea is that while the magnitude of the tension is the same at both ends (since we assume constant tension), the direction can be slightly different since the string might be curved.

Each tension vector can be broken into horizontal and vertical components. The vertical component of the tension at position $x$ is given by

$$T_y(x) = T\sin{\theta}(x)\,, \tag{D1.1}$$

where $\theta(x)$ is the angle the string makes with the horizontal at that point. If the slope of the string is small, we can use the approximation $\sin{\theta} \approx \tan{\theta} = \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}$. So the vertical component becomes

$$T_y(x) \approx T\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x,t)\,. \tag{D1.2}$$

Similarly, the vertical component at the other end, $x + \Delta x$, is

$$T_y(x + \Delta x) \approx T\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x + \Delta x, t)\,. \tag{D1.3}$$

The net vertical force on the segment is the difference between these two vertical components:

$$F_y = T\left[ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x + \Delta x, t) - \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x,t) \right]\,. \tag{D1.4}$$

Now, if $\Delta x$ is very small, the difference in slope becomes a derivative:

$$F_y = T\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}\Delta x\,. \tag{D1.5}$$

This is the net vertical force acting on the segment of the string due to the way the slope (and thus the vertical component of tension) changes along the string.

Next, we apply Newton's second law to this segment. The mass of the small piece of string is $\mu\Delta x$, and its vertical acceleration is $\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial t^2}$, so:

$$\mu\Delta x \frac{\partial^2y}{\partial t^2} = T\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}\Delta x\,. \tag{D1.6}$$

Canceling the $\Delta x$ and dividing both sides by $\mu$, we get:

$$\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial t^2} = \frac{T}{\mu}\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}\,. \tag{D1.7}$$

This is the wave equation for a vibrating string. It tells us that the acceleration of a small piece of string depends on the curvature of the string at that point; if the string is curved, tension pulls it back toward being straight.

We define the wave speed as

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}\,, \tag{D1.8}$$

which leads to the final form of the wave equation:

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial t^2} = v^2\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}}\,. \tag{D1.9}$$

A solution to this equation for a harmonic wave traveling in the positive $x$ direction with wave number $k$ and angular frequency $\omega$ is

$$\begin{equation} y(x,t) = A\sin{\left( kx - \omega t \right)} \,.\label{eq:solution-1} \end{equation}$$

One way to produce a standing wave is by adding two identical traveling waves moving in opposite directions. You can explore this by adjusting the properties of the waves in the interactive widget below.

The equivalent of Equation $\ref{eq:solution-1}$ for a wave traveling in the negative $x$ direction with the same wave number and angular frequency is

$$\begin{equation} y(x,t) = A\sin{\left( kx + \omega t \right)} \,.\label{eq:solution-2} \end{equation}$$

The sum of these two waves is then

$$\begin{equation} y(x,t) = A[\sin{(kx-\omega t)}+\sin{(kx + \omega t)}]\,.\label{eq:wave-sum} \end{equation}$$

Using the identity for the sum of sines, this becomes

$$\begin{equation} y(x,t) = 2A\sin{(kx)}\cos{(\omega t)} \,.\label{eq:identity} \end{equation}$$

This represents a standing wave: a spatial sine function multiplied by a time-dependent cosine factor. The amplitude at each position $x$ oscillates in time but does not propagate along the string.

For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends ($x=0$ and $x=L$), the displacement must vanish at these points for all times. The boundary condition at $x=0$ is automatically satisfied by $\sin{(kx)}$, since $\sin{(0)}=0$. The second condition, $y(L,t)=0$, requires

$$\begin{equation} \sin{(kL)}=0 \,,\label{eq:bc} \end{equation}$$

which holds when

$$\begin{equation} kL=n\pi, \hspace{0.25cm} \mathrm{where} \hspace{0.15cm} n=1,2,3,... \label{eq:kL} \end{equation}$$

Thus, the allowed wave numbers are

$$\begin{equation} k = \frac{n\pi}{L} \hspace{0.25cm} \mathrm{where} \hspace{0.15cm} n=1,2,3,... \label{eq:wavenumbers} \end{equation}$$

The value $n$ is called the harmonic number. A standing wave with $n$ antinodes is said to be oscillating in its $n$-th harmonic.

The wave speed $v$ on a string under tension $T$ with linear mass density $\mu$ is given by

$$\begin{equation} v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} \,.\label{eq:velocity} \end{equation}$$

This wave speed also relates frequency $f$ and wave number by

$$\begin{equation} v = \lambda f = \frac{2\pi f}{k} \,.\label{eq:vwk} \end{equation}$$

Substituting Equation $\ref{eq:wavenumbers}$ for $k$ and Equation $\ref{eq:velocity}$ for $v$,

$$\begin{equation} \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} = 2\pi f \cdot \frac{L}{n\pi} = \frac{2Lf}{n} \,.\label{eq:frequency} \end{equation}$$

The easiest dependent variable to measure in an experiment involving standing waves on a string is the number of antinodes $n$. Solving for this,

$$\begin{equation} \boxed{n = 2Lf\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{T}}} \,.\label{eq:n} \end{equation}$$

Pre-Lab Questions

Submit your responses to the following questions by the start of the relevant lab meeting.

  1. Prove by direct substitution that Equation $\ref{eq:solution-1}$ satisfies the wave equation.

  2. A standing wave is created on a string, driven by some frequency $f$. Now suppose this frequency is doubled. Would the resultant wave still be a standing wave? If so, by what factor is its wavelength changed?

  3. A standing wave is created on a string of tension $T$. Now suppose this tension is doubled. Would the resultant wave still be a standing wave? If so, by what factor is its wavelength changed?

  4. A frequency of $440\,\mathrm{Hz}$ is used to vibrate one end of a $20\,\mathrm{cm}$ string with linear mass density $1\,\mathrm{mg/cm}$, and a weight of $35\,\mathrm{g}$ is suspended from the other end of this string. A standing wave is produced. In what harmonic does this standing wave oscillate?

  5. A standing wave is generated at frequency $f$ on a string of length $L$, linear mass density $\mu$, and tension $T$. To increase the frequency of the standing wave, how should each of the following parameters be changed: $L$, $\mu$, and $T$? For each one, state whether it should be increased, decreased, or left unchanged, and explain why.

Procedure

Measure the mass density of a string of unknown material by varying the string tension to generate standing waves and counting their antinodes.
